21 Lessons from 21 Months at McKinsey

May 31, 2025· Ricardo Santana

A. First Principles

B. Solve Systematically

C. Communicate Clearly

D. Execute Flawlessly

A. First Principles

1. You can seat at any table

Build the confidence and competence to contribute meaningfully in any business context, regardless of your background or experience level.

2. Always have a point of view

Never show up empty-handed. Form hypotheses based on available data and give stakeholders something concrete to react to, even when information is incomplete.

3. Feedback is a gift

Embrace all feedback as an opportunity to improve. The best consultants actively seek input and view criticism as valuable intelligence.

4. First impressions matter

You have one chance to establish credibility. Prepare meticulously, dress appropriately, and bring your A-game from day one.

5. The world runs on relationships

Success depends as much on how well you work with others as what you produce. Invest time in building genuine professional relationships.

6. Growth is out of your comfort zone

Continuously seek challenges that stretch your capabilities. The steepest learning curve is where the most valuable growth happens.

B. Solve Systematically

7. Structure before solving

Structure your thinking using Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive frameworks. Every analysis should be complete and without overlap.

8. Start with a solution

Start with your best guess about the answer, then design analysis to prove or disprove it. This focuses effort and accelerates insight generation.

9. Regular triangle

Balance three key elements in every recommendation: what’s right for the business, what’s feasible to implement, and what stakeholders will accept.

10. 80/20 rule

Focus on the 20% of activities that drive 80% of the impact. Perfectionism is the enemy of progress and client value.

11. Feasibility-Impact matrix

Prioritize initiatives by plotting them on impact vs. ease of implementation. Focus on high-impact, high-feasibility opportunities first.

C. Communicate Clearly

12. Start with the headline

Begin with the big picture and work down to details, not the reverse. This ensures you’re solving the right problem before diving into specifics.

13. People love stories

Every presentation needs a clear narrative arc. Know your key message and structure everything to support that central story.

14. Format kills content

How you present information is as important as the information itself. Poor formatting kills good ideas.

15. One slide, one message

Each slide should convey one clear, complete message. If you can’t explain it simply, you don’t understand it well enough.

16. 3-bullet rule

Limit key points to three per slide or section. The human brain processes information in groups of three most effectively.

D. Execute Flawlessly

17. Pick the low hanging fruit

Identify and pursue easy victories early to build momentum and credibility while working on longer-term solutions.

18. Deadlines drive progress

Master project planning tools and understand that work expands to fill available time. Set aggressive but achievable deadlines.

19. Make productive meetings

Every meeting needs a purpose, structure, and clear outcomes. Respect everyone’s time by running tight, productive sessions.

20. Underpromise, overdeliver

Set realistic expectations and then exceed them. Your reputation is built on consistently delivering more than promised.

21. Important decisions are never made on the spot

Socialize key recommendations with stakeholders individually before formal presentations. Build alignment privately to ensure public success.
